This guide provides an overview of rental fundamentals in Sydney, including inspections, applications, lease terms, bonds, and tenant rights. It is intended to help renters understand the key steps and considerations when securing a rental property.

Rental Inspections:
Most rental properties require an in‑person or virtual inspection before applying. Inspections are typically scheduled on weekends or weekday evenings. Prospective tenants should check the condition of appliances, water pressure, ventilation, natural light, and any signs of mould or damage. It is common for multiple groups to attend the same inspection, especially in high‑demand suburbs.

Rental Applications:
Applications are usually submitted through platforms such as realestate.com.au, Domain, or agency portals. Required documents typically include identification, proof of income, recent payslips, bank statements, rental history, and references. Strong applications often include a brief introduction letter and evidence of stable employment. Competition can be high in inner‑city and high‑amenity suburbs.

Lease Terms:
Most leases in Sydney are for 6 or 12 months. A 12‑month lease often provides more stability, while a 6‑month lease offers flexibility. Leases specify rent amount, payment frequency, included utilities (if any), and rules regarding pets, modifications, and subletting. Tenants should carefully review all clauses before signing.

Rental Bond:
A rental bond is a security deposit paid at the start of the lease, usually equivalent to 4 weeks’ rent. The bond is lodged with the NSW Rental Bond Board and is refundable at the end of the tenancy, provided there is no damage beyond fair wear and tear. Tenants should complete a condition report at the start of the lease to avoid disputes later.

Rent Payments:
Rent is typically paid weekly or fortnightly via direct debit or transfer. Late payments can affect the tenancy and may lead to breach notices. Some landlords offer rent discounts for longer leases or upfront payments, though this is less common.

Condition Report:
Before moving in, tenants must complete a condition report documenting the state of the property. This includes photos of walls, floors, appliances, and any existing damage. The condition report is essential for protecting the bond at the end of the lease.

Repairs and Maintenance:
Landlords are responsible for major repairs, structural issues, and essential services such as plumbing, electricity, and heating. Tenants are responsible for minor upkeep and cleanliness. Urgent repairs, such as burst pipes or electrical faults, must be addressed promptly by the landlord or agent.

Rent Increases:
Rent increases are allowed during periodic leases and at the end of fixed‑term leases. Increases must be reasonable and follow NSW tenancy regulations. Tenants must receive written notice, typically at least 60 days before the increase takes effect.

Ending a Lease:
Tenants can end a lease by giving proper notice, usually 21 days for periodic leases or 14 days before the end of a fixed‑term lease. Breaking a lease early may incur fees, often calculated as a percentage of the remaining rent. Tenants should check the break‑lease clause in their agreement.

Inspections During Tenancy:
Landlords may conduct routine inspections, usually every 3–6 months. Tenants must receive written notice before an inspection. Inspections are used to check property condition and identify maintenance needs.

Common Rental Costs:
In addition to rent, tenants may need to budget for utilities such as electricity, gas, water usage, and internet. Some apartments include water usage in the rent, while others bill separately. Strata‑managed buildings may have rules regarding move‑in fees or lift bookings.

Tenant Rights:
Tenants in NSW have the right to a safe and habitable property, privacy, and timely repairs. Landlords must provide notice before entering the property and cannot evict tenants without proper legal process. Disputes can be resolved through NSW Fair Trading or the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT).

Overall Summary:
Renting in Sydney involves understanding inspections, applications, lease terms, bonds, and tenant rights. Preparation, strong documentation, and awareness of local market conditions can significantly improve the chances of securing a suitable rental property.
