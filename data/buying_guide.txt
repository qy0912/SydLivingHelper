This guide provides an overview of the key concepts involved in buying property in Sydney, including loan preparation, inspections, contracts, settlement, and common terminology. It is intended to help buyers understand the major steps and considerations when purchasing a home or investment property.

Loan Pre‑Approval:
Most buyers begin the process by obtaining loan pre‑approval from a bank or mortgage broker. Pre‑approval indicates how much a lender is willing to loan based on income, savings, and credit history. It typically lasts 3–6 months and helps buyers set a realistic budget. Pre‑approval is essential for participating in auctions, where unconditional offers are required.

Deposit and Down Payment:
In Sydney, buyers typically need a minimum 20% deposit to avoid lenders mortgage insurance (LMI). Some lenders allow deposits as low as 5–10%, but this increases borrowing costs. Savings, gifts, and government schemes may contribute to the deposit. A higher deposit improves loan approval chances and reduces interest expenses.

Stamp Duty:
Stamp duty is a government tax paid when purchasing property. The amount depends on the property price and buyer category. First‑home buyers may receive concessions or exemptions depending on eligibility and property value. Stamp duty must be paid shortly after settlement unless deferred under specific schemes.

Property Types:
Sydney properties fall into several categories:
- Freehold: The buyer owns the land and the building.
- Strata: The buyer owns the unit interior and shares ownership of common areas.
- Torrens Title: The buyer owns both the land and dwelling without strata fees.
- Company Title: Ownership is through shares in a company that controls the building.
Strata properties typically have ongoing strata fees for maintenance and building management.

Strata Fees:
Strata fees cover building insurance, maintenance, cleaning, and shared facilities such as lifts, pools, and gyms. Fees vary widely depending on building age and amenities. Buyers should review the strata report to understand financial health, upcoming repairs, and any special levies.

Buying Methods:
Sydney properties are sold through two main methods: auction and private treaty.

Auction:
Auctions are common in Sydney, especially for houses and high‑demand suburbs. Buyers must have finance ready, as auction purchases are unconditional. The highest bidder above the reserve price wins. A 10% deposit is usually paid immediately after the auction. Cooling‑off periods do not apply to auction purchases.

Private Treaty:
In private treaty sales, buyers submit offers to the agent. Negotiation is possible, and buyers may include conditions such as finance approval or building inspections. A cooling‑off period of five business days typically applies unless waived. Buyers pay a small holding deposit when the offer is accepted.

Building and Pest Inspection:
For houses and townhouses, buyers often arrange a building and pest inspection before committing to the purchase. The inspection identifies structural issues, water damage, pests, and potential repair costs. For strata properties, buyers should review the strata report to understand building condition and financial status.

Contract Review:
Before signing, buyers should have a solicitor or conveyancer review the contract of sale. The review covers title details, zoning, easements, inclusions, settlement terms, and any special conditions. Legal advice helps identify risks and negotiate contract changes.

Cooling‑Off Period:
Private treaty purchases include a cooling‑off period during which buyers may withdraw from the contract. A small penalty fee applies. Auctions do not include cooling‑off periods, and contracts become binding immediately.

Settlement:
Settlement is the final stage of the purchase, typically occurring 4–6 weeks after signing the contract. During settlement, the buyer pays the remaining balance, stamp duty is processed, and ownership transfers. Buyers receive the keys once settlement is complete.

First Home Buyer Schemes:
NSW offers several schemes for eligible first‑home buyers, including stamp duty concessions, grants for new homes, and shared equity programs. Eligibility depends on income, property value, and residency status. Buyers should check current government guidelines before applying.

Price Trends and Market Conditions:
Sydney property prices vary significantly by suburb, property type, and market cycle. Buyers should consider long‑term growth trends, rental demand, infrastructure projects, and demographic changes. Understanding historical price movements helps assess value and future potential.

Overall Summary:
Buying property in Sydney involves understanding loan preparation, inspections, contracts, and settlement. Careful planning, legal review, and awareness of market conditions can help buyers make informed decisions and secure a suitable property.
